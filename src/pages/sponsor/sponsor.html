Coffee Mine Project



More than Gold: Our approach in the Yukon

We want to leave a positive, lasting impression in the Yukon. We’ll do that by hiring qualified Yukoners and First Nations members wherever possible, and by providing training, education and career development opportunities for our employees. For example, we’re partnering with Yukon College to support local training and career development opportunities.


Partnering with First Nations

Our Whitehorse-based project team is working closely with First Nations and community partners to design a socially and environmentally responsible project. A Collaboration Agreement between the Tr’ondëk Hwëch’in and Goldcorp represents an important milestone towards building a long-term relationship with local First Nations and delivering economic opportunities at each stage of the project.


Treating the environment with care

The land of the Yukon is unique, and requires special care by those working on it – at every stage of the mining life cycle. Our project will be designed in accordance with Newmont Goldcorp’s environmental standards, which are designed to protect the environment and prevent – or otherwise minimize, mitigate and remediate – those impacts. A wildlife monitoring program is being developed to ensure our operations don’t harm wildlife such as bears, moose and caribou, and our Project will adhere to the International Cyanide Management Code.


Project Description

The Project contains a substantial oxide resource and will be a conventional, open pit and heap leach mine with no tailings. Site infrastructure features four open pits with one rock storage facility.

Mine life includes 2.5 years of construction, 8-10 years of operational mine life, and 10-11 years of closure, with progressive reclamation beginning early in the operational mine life.


Conceptual design indicates approximately 60 million tonnes of mined ore over the mine life, producing over 2.5 M oz au and expected to grow as near pit exploration continues.


The Project also includes an all-weather access road, referred to as the Northern Access Route (NAR), which is approximately 214 km long. The NAR follows existing public roads for over 80% of the route and will required approximately 37 km of new road construction, along with various upgrades along portions of the remainder of the route.

Site access

The Project will be a fly-in, fly-out site, with supplies coming in on the NAR from Dawson. The NAR is a proposed 214-kilometre, all-weather road beginning at the Hunker Road.

  1. Barges and ice roads will be used to cross the Stewart and Yukon Rivers along the NAR. As a result, there will be a portion of the year where the site is only accessible by air, during the freeze up and thaw periods; current estimates account for approximately 15 weeks in the fall and 15 weeks in the spring.

  2. Currently, the Coffee Exploration Program is accessed primarily by air, with some supplies coming in by barge when water levels on the Yukon River are high enough.

Job opportunities

  1. Approximately 400 direct jobs will be created by the Project during the peak of the construction phase.

  2. Approximately 340 direct jobs will be created by the Project during the operation phase over a number of shifts. Direct, indirect and induced jobs expected to be approximately 400-430 during operations.

Key Development Milestones

  1. Initiated Environmental and Socioeconomic Assessment Dec. 2017 with Yukon Environmental and Socio-Economic Assessment Board (YESAB).

  2. Tr'ondëk Hwëch'in and Newmont Goldcorp Sign Collaboration Agreement – completed in 2018


Project Schedule

The Coffee Mine Project is currently in the screening stage of the YESAB Executive Committee assessment process. Once the Decision Document is issued by YESAB, the Coffee Mine Project will apply for a Quartz Mining License and a Type A Water Use License. Construction and operations cannot commence until permits are in-hand for either the Northern Access Route or the mine.
